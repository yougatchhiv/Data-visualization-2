Beneath the Surface: The Reef in Numbers

Three decades of reef health surveys and ten years of visitor records, brought together to tell a story of resilience, pressure, and uncertain recovery across the world's largest coral reef system. Data: AIMS Long-Term Monitoring Program (1993-2023) and GBRMPA Permit Holder Reporting System (2016-2025).

The GBRMP spans Queensland's coastline from Cape York to Bundaberg, organised into four management areas and eleven monitoring sectors. Each year, AIMS researchers conduct standardised manta-tow surveys, slow passes above a reef transect, recording live coral, dead coral, and predator counts.

Millions of visitors, Five Type of Experience

Visitor access to the GBRMP is recorded through a permit system. Full-day trips dominate, accounting for roughly 66% of recorded visits. The chart below reveals the sharp discontinuity of 2020: COVID-19 border closures reduced annual visitation by 66%, from 2.4 million to under one million. Recovery has been steady but uneven ever since.

source: GBRMPA Permit Holder Reporting System (2016-2025)

At its peak in 2016, the reef had 2.79 million visitors across all permit types, the highest ever recorded. That number collapsed dramatically in 2020 when COVID-19 border closures reduced visitation to just 956,000, a 66% decline. By 2025 the industry had recovered largely, with 2.38 million visitors returning to the reef. Of those 2025 visitors, 218,000 held coral-viewing permits covering snorkelling and diving activities making them the segment most directly dependent on a healthy, living reef but this would also implies that if there were another major decline, they are the most vulnerable to the change making tourism directly related to the wellbeing of these reefs.

At the sub-regional level, Cairns/Cooktown and Townsville/Whitsunday together account for over 90% of all visitor activity. The Far Northern Area records fewer than 15,000 annual visits due to its remoteness from the Queensland coast.

source: GBRMPA Permit Holder Reporting System (2016-2025)

Coral Under Pressure: Thirty Years of Monitoring

AIMS has surveyed the reef since 1993, producing the world’s longest-running coral reef health time series. The trend is not a simple decline — it is a story of stress and recovery, repeated at shorter and shorter intervals. Each mass bleaching event leaves the reef with less time to bounce back before the next thermal anomaly arrives.

source: GBRMPA Permit Holder Reporting System (2016-2025)

Key finding: Between 2020 and 2022 the reef recorded its highest measured coral cover since monitoring began — 35.5% — before a fourth bleaching event in early 2022 interrupted that recovery.

This timeline illustrates the escalating severity of mass coral bleaching events on the Great Barrier Reef across three distinct periods. The first events in 1998 and 2002 were driven by El Niño-induced ocean warming, but the reef demonstrated a capacity for recovery during the gaps between them. The situation worsened dramatically in 2016 and 2017 when back-to-back bleaching events — the worst ever recorded — struck the northern sectors in consecutive years, destroying roughly half the coral in those regions with no recovery time in between. The most alarming development came in 2020 and 2022, when bleaching occurred during a La Niña period — traditionally associated with cooler ocean temperatures that give the reef relief — signalling that the reef has crossed a dangerous new threshold of vulnerability where even historically safe climate conditions can no longer be relied upon to prevent bleaching.
