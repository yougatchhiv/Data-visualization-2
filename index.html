Beneath the Surface: The Reef in Numbers

Three decades of reef health surveys and ten years of visitor records, brought together to tell a story of resilience, pressure, and uncertain recovery across the world's largest coral reef system.

The Great Barrier Reef Monitoring Plan (GBRMP) cover Queensland's coastline from Cape York all the way to Bundaberg, totaling up to 11 sectors that gets monitored yearly. Each year the Australia Institute of Marine Science (AIMS) researcher conduct manta-tow survey for each sections and record the situation of the corals within the reefs.

The map shows the average live coral cover per reef from the year 2018 to 2023. The color scale represent the conditions of the corals where being in the red zone (less than 10%) signal possible poor living conditions, dead corals, algae and being in the green zone (more than 40%) would signal the opposite.

During 2016, there were a bleaching event that had caused the coral to starve and die due to events of el nino and climate change. source (Di Liberto, T 2016)

The map shows that the average corals tend to be better the further it is from the coast, while this is true as the further from the coast, the living conditions of the corals would increase greatly, however, during the el nino year these corals are actually the most vulnerable to the increase in temperature.

source: AIMS (2023)

Coral Under Pressure: Thirty Years of Monitoring

There were 30 years worth of data recorded by the Australia Institute of Marine Science started since 1993 which is also the world's longest coral reef health time series. The trend shows the decline and recovering of the average live corals within this time as well as the effects of other bleaching events. Each mass bleaching event leaves the reef with less time to bounce back before the next thermal anomaly arrives.

source: GBRMPA Permit Holder Reporting System (2016-2025)

Key finding: The bleaching events occurred in 1998, 2002, 2016 and 2017 and 2 more in 2020 and 2022.Between 2020 and 2022, the reef recorded the highest measured coral covers before yet another bleaching event occurred that interrupted and caused yet another decline.

This timeline illustrates the escalating severity of mass coral bleaching events on the Great Barrier Reef across three distinct periods. The first events in 1998 and 2002 were driven by El Niño-induced ocean warming, but the reef demonstrated a capacity for recovery during the gaps between them. The situation worsened dramatically in 2016 and 2017 when back-to-back bleaching events — the worst ever recorded — struck the northern sectors in consecutive years, destroying roughly half the coral in those regions with no recovery time in between. The most alarming development came in 2020 and 2022, when bleaching occurred during a La Niña period — traditionally associated with cooler ocean temperatures that give the reef relief — signalling that the reef has crossed a dangerous new threshold of vulnerability where even historically safe climate conditions can no longer be relied upon to prevent bleaching.

The Full Picture: Distribution and Disruption

We can look further into the three shelfs that the survey had recorded for the percentage of coral cover, we find that there are no uniform changes for all three sections, there is a situation where when the number of outer shelf increase the number of inner shelf and mid shelf decreases. This shows that different measures are required to maintain and conserve different sections of the reef.

source: GBRMPA Permit Holder Reporting System (2016-2025)

Inner-shelf reefs growth are suppressed by coastal runoff and agricultural sediment. Outer-shelf reefs carry higher cover on average but are most exposed to open-ocean warming that drives bleaching.

This graph shows the difference in growth over the years as well as how each sections recovers differently after the bleaching event in 2016. It is obvious that the outer shelf would have much more growth due to the open ocean, cleaner and clearer environment to prosper but they are also the one to suffer the most during every bleaching event.

Millions of visitors, Five Type of Experience

There are millions of visitors that comes to the Great Barrier Reef annually, according to the visitors data recorded by GBRMP between 2016 to 2025, over half of the recorded visitors are those that does full day trips, peaking at 2.79 million visitors in 2016 alone. However, we can also find that there is a sharp decrease in 2020 before the number of visitors climb back up in 2022. This is because of the covid pandemic. The number of visitors continue to recover as the year goes by.

source: GBRMPA Permit Holder Reporting System (2016-2025)

At its peak in 2016, the reef had 2.79 million visitors across all permit types, the highest ever recorded. That number collapsed dramatically in 2020 when COVID-19 border closures reduced visitation to just 956,000, a 66% decline. By 2025 the industry had recovered largely, with 2.38 million visitors returning to the reef. Of those 2025 visitors, 218,000 held coral-viewing permits covering snorkelling and diving activities making them the segment most directly dependent on a healthy, living reef but this would also implies that if there were another major decline, they are the most vulnerable to the change making tourism directly related to the wellbeing of these reefs.

Monitoring the. coral reefs is not only important is conserving one of nature's beautiful creation but it also serve as a popular tourism spot for millions other to come and visit which in turn help provide a better support in maintaining the ecological system of the corals.

Looking at the different regions, we find that most visits happens within Townsville/Whitsunday and Cairns/Cooktown throughout the year 2016 to 2025.

Full-day trips represent 66% of all 2025 visits. Coral-viewing permits represent 9.2% and are the category most directly dependent on a healthy reef to attract bookings.

Tourism and Reef Health : An Unexpected Relationship

We can look at the correlation between the number of tourisms and the health of the reef to see if there are any major impacts in a negative or positive effects. While the number of corals reef did increase during the covid pandemic in 2020 where the number of tourism has declined the most but it has also started recovering along with the increase with tourisms afterward as well. This does not establish a causation for the reef's health, so there are no concerning relationships that would be considered as an issue.

source: GBRMPA Permit Holder Reporting System (2016-2025)

Each labelled point represents one year, with the path tracing chronological order. Follow the line from 2016 rightward as visitors grow and coral declines, then watch the sharp left turn in 2020 as COVID collapses tourism — while coral simultaneously rebounds to its highest recorded level.

By 2023, visitors were recovering and coral remained relatively healthy, suggesting the relationship is more complex than a simple cause-and-effect.
