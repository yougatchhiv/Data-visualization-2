Part 1

Meet the Reef

The Great Barrier Reef is the world's largest coral ecosystem — a living structure so vast it can be seen from space.

The reef is divided into three broad management zones — North, Central, and South — each with distinct ecological conditions, water temperatures, and levels of human activity.

The northern section is the most remote and historically the most pristine. It was also the hardest hit by the 2016 and 2017 bleaching events, losing more than two-thirds of its shallow-water coral in two consecutive summers.

"The most diverse and complex ecosystem on Earth is in serious trouble."

Part 2

A Reef Under Threat

Since monitoring began in 1985, coral cover has fallen sharply — and the pace of decline is accelerating.

Hard coral cover — the structural foundation of the reef — peaked in the late 1980s and has been in near-continuous decline since.

The steepest drops coincide with six mass bleaching events since 1998, five of which occurred in the last decade alone.

Part 3

Six Summers That Changed Everything

Mass bleaching events have gone from rare occurrences to near-annual crises.

Bleaching occurs when water temperatures rise just 1–2°C above the seasonal average for an extended period, causing corals to expel their symbiotic algae and turn white.

A bleached coral is not dead — but without recovery time it will starve within weeks. The back-to-back bleaching in 2016 and 2017 gave northern reef corals no chance to recover between events.

By 2022, bleaching had been recorded in five of the past seven summers — a frequency scientists had not expected until the 2030s.

Part 4

What's Driving the Decline

Temperature is the primary driver — but it acts in concert with other stressors.

Part 5

The Human Cost

The reef's decline is not just an ecological crisis — it threatens the livelihoods of tens of thousands of Australians who depend on it.

More than 2 million people visit the reef each year, generating over $6 billion in revenue and supporting 64,000 jobs across regional Queensland.

Tourism operators in Cairns, Port Douglas, and the Whitsundays report growing concern as repeat visitors describe a noticeably degraded experience compared to trips a decade ago.

"A severely degraded reef could cost Queensland up to $1 billion annually in lost tourism revenue."
